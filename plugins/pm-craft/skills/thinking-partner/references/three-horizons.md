# Three Horizons Framework

A framework for thinking about the future and managing innovation/transformation over time. It helps distinguish between sustaining the current business and growing future opportunities.

## The Three Horizons

### Horizon 1 (H1): The Current System ("Business as Usual")
-   **Focus**: Managing the existing core business/system.
-   **Mindset**: Managerial. Efficiency, stability, profit maximization.
-   **Risk**: Being disrupted by change; holding on too long.
-   **Key Question**: What is dying or needs to be maintained for now?

### Horizon 2 (H2): The Transition Zone ("Turbulence")
-   **Focus**: Innovations that disrupt H1 but aren't yet fully H3.
-   **Mindset**: Entrepreneurial. Experimentation, friction, bridge-building.
-   **Risk**: "H2-" (captured by H1 to sustain the old) vs "H2+" (paving the way to H3).
-   **Key Question**: What innovations are resolving the tension between H1 and H3?

### Horizon 3 (H3): The Viable Future ("Vision")
-   **Focus**: Long-term future emerging from fringe changes.
-   **Mindset**: Visionary. Possibility, values, completely new paradigms.
-   **Risk**: Utopian dreaming without connection to reality.
-   **Key Question**: What is the ideal future system we want to create?

## Usage in Foresight

When acting as a thinking partner, use this framework to categorise the user's thoughts and prompts:
1.  **Identify H1**: "What are the current constraints or 'business as usual' assumptions?"
2.  **Clarify H3**: "If you could start from scratch, what would the ideal system look like?"
3.  **Explore H2**: "What initiatives or bridges can take us from H1 to H3?"

## Bill Sharpe's Patterning
-   **H1 Manager** sees H2 as risky and H3 as irrelevant.
-   **H2 Entrepreneur** sees H1 as obstructive and H3 as resource-poor.
-   **H3 Visionary** sees H1 as obsolete and H2 as a compromise.
**Goal**: Facilitate dialogue between these voices.
