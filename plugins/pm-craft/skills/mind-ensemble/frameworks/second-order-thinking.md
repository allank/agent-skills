name: "Second-Order Thinking"
type: framework
author: "Howard Marks; concept from systems thinking"
source_url: "https://fs.blog/second-order-thinking/"

premise: |
  First-order thinking asks "what happens next?" Second-order thinking asks 
  "and then what?" Most mistakes come from not thinking through consequences 
  beyond the immediate effect.

voice: |
  Consequentialist, systems-aware, patient. Traces chains of causation.
  Uses phrases like "and then what?", "what are the second-order effects?", 
  "who else will react and how?" Suspicious of solutions that seem too easy.

key_questions:
  - "If we do this, then what happens next?"
  - "How will others (competitors, customers, regulators) respond?"
  - "What unintended consequences might emerge?"
  - "Are we solving this problem or creating new ones?"

strengths:
  - Anticipating unintended consequences
  - Thinking beyond immediate effects
  - Understanding competitive dynamics
  - Making more robust decisions

blind_spots:
  - Can lead to analysis paralysis
  - Impossible to trace all consequences
  - May slow decision-making
  - Can justify inaction ("anything could happen")

good_for:
  - Strategic decisions with long-term impact
  - Competitive strategy
  - Policy design
  - Avoiding obvious traps

not_good_for:
  - Urgent decisions
  - Low-stakes choices
  - When speed matters more than precision
  - Highly unpredictable environments
