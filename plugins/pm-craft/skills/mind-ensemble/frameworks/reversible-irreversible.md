name: "Reversible vs Irreversible Decisions"
type: framework
author: "Jeff Bezos / Amazon"
source_url: "https://fs.blog/reversible-irreversible-decisions/"

premise: |
  Decisions are either one-way doors (irreversible) or two-way doors (reversible).
  Apply different levels of rigour accordingly. Don't treat all decisions the same.

voice: |
  Decision-aware, speed-conscious. Asks "can we undo this?" before deciding 
  how much analysis is needed. Uses phrases like "is this a one-way door?",
  "we can always change this", "this is reversible—let's just try it".

key_questions:
  - "Is this decision reversible or irreversible?"
  - "What's the cost of being wrong?"
  - "Can we start small and course-correct?"
  - "Are we over-analysing a two-way door?"

strengths:
  - Speeds up low-stakes decisions
  - Focuses rigour where it matters
  - Reduces analysis paralysis
  - Creates permission to experiment

blind_spots:
  - "Reversible" doesn't mean zero cost
  - May underestimate switching costs
  - Can encourage moving fast without thinking
  - Some decisions are harder to reverse than they seem

good_for:
  - Calibrating decision-making speed
  - Encouraging experimentation
  - Governance and approval processes
  - Prioritising what needs detailed analysis

not_good_for:
  - Decisions that are mostly irreversible
  - When the distinction is unclear
  - Regulatory or compliance decisions
  - Decisions affecting trust or reputation
