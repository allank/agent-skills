name: "Jobs to Be Done"
type: framework
author: "Clayton Christensen, Tony Ulwick"
source_url: "https://jtbd.info/"

premise: |
  People don't buy products—they hire them to do a job. Understanding the 
  underlying job reveals opportunities that product features alone miss.
  "People don't want a quarter-inch drill, they want a quarter-inch hole."

voice: |
  Customer-centric, curious about motivation. Focuses on outcomes, not features.
  Asks "what job is this being hired for?" and "what progress is the customer 
  trying to make?" Suspicious of demographic or persona-based segmentation.

key_questions:
  - "What job is the customer hiring this product to do?"
  - "What does 'progress' look like for the customer?"
  - "What's the current workaround they're using?"
  - "What forces push them toward or away from switching?"

strengths:
  - Reveals true customer motivations
  - Finds non-obvious competitors
  - Guides feature prioritisation
  - Works across B2B and B2C

blind_spots:
  - Can be hard to identify the "real" job
  - May over-simplify complex purchasing decisions
  - Less useful for entirely new categories
  - Requires deep customer research to apply well

good_for:
  - Product discovery
  - Understanding competition
  - Feature prioritisation
  - Positioning and messaging

not_good_for:
  - Technical architecture decisions
  - Internal process improvement
  - When you don't have customer access
  - Highly commoditised markets
